### **SLIDE 2: Key Principle: Timing is Everything**
- **Golden rule:** Antibiotics **within 1 hour of injury** (or within 1 hour of arrival if delayed presentation) [30][31][38]
- **Rationale:** Reduces surgical site infection (SSI) by ~30% [30][31]
- **Multiinstitutional ** When 1-hour goal met, infection rates drop from 12% → 8.5% [31][38]
- **Reality check:** Current median time to antibiotics = 69–90 minutes (above 1-hour goal) [31]
- **Barrier:** Prolonged pre-hospital times in penetrating injuries delay administration [38]
- *Evidence: AAST 2024, EAST 2024, surgical infection literature*

***

### **SLIDE 3: Duration Rules: Less is More**
- **Standard trauma (most injuries):** ≤24 hours prophylaxis (no post-op antibiotics after 24 h) [30][31][38]
- **Extended (select injuries):**
  - **Penetrating CNS injury:** Brief course only (24–48 h) [30]
  - **Intra-abdominal contamination (iatrogenic):** 24 h post-op [30]
  - **Facial fractures (dentate mandible):** Until fixation + 24 h post-op [38]
- **Non-operative injuries:** **NO prophylactic antibiotics** (close fractures, simple lacerations) [38]
- **Pitfall:** 75% of surgeons over-prescribe (continue >24 h unnecessarily) [38]
- *Evidence: AAST guidelines, scoping reviews, antibiotic stewardship*

***

### **SLIDE 4: Antibiotics for Open Fractures: AAST Algorithm**
| **Open Fracture Grade** | **Antibiotic Regimen** | **Duration** | **Start** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I (minimal contamination)** | First-generation cephalosporin | ≤24 h | <1 h |
| **II (moderate contamination)** | 1st-gen cephalosporin + gentamicin | ≤24 h | <1 h |
| **III (severe/soil/marine)** | 1st-gen ceph + gentamicin **+ clindamycin** (anaerobes) | ≤24 h | <1 h |
| **III with vascular injury** | Same + operative urgency | ≤24 h | <1 h |

- **Dosing:** Cefazolin 1–2 g IV (2 g if >120 kg) [30][38]
- **Renal dosing:** Adjust gentamicin for CrCl [30]
- *Evidence: AAST 2024, Gustilo & Anderson classification*

***

### **SLIDE 5: Penetrating Abdominal Trauma**
- **Indication:** All penetrating abdominal/flank wounds [30]
- **Antibiotic choice:** Cefoxitin (covers Gram-neg + anaerobes) OR Ampicillin-sulbactam [30]
- **Duration:** **≤24 hours** if no therapeutic laparotomy findings [30]
  - If contamination found (bowel perforation): Treat as complicated intra-abdominal infection (longer course) [30]
- **Timing:** Within 1 hour [30]
- **Update 2024:** No difference in outcomes between 24 h vs 48 h prophylaxis [30]
- *Evidence: AAST 2024 trauma guidelines*

***

### **SLIDE 6: Penetrating CNS Injury (Head/Spine)**
- **Indication:** All penetrating head/spine injuries [30]
- **Concern:** Meningitis risk (CSF leak, foreign body) [30]
- **Antibiotic choice:** **Ceftriaxone 2 g IV q12h** (better CNS penetration than cefazolin) [30][38]
- **Duration:** **Brief course only (24–48 h)** then stop [30]
  - Prolonged antibiotics don't prevent infection; increase resistance [30]
- **Timing:** Within 1 hour [30]
- **Follow-up:** Monitor for meningitis signs (fever, nuchal rigidity, altered mental status) [30]
- *Evidence: AAST 2024*

***

### **SLIDE 7: Facial Fractures – Traditional Approach**
- **Closed (non-operative) fractures:** **NO antibiotics** [38]
- **Operative facial fractures:** Perioperative prophylaxis only [38]
  - Upper/mid-face (frontal, zygomatic, nasal): Single preop dose cefazolin [38]
  - **Dentate mandible (angle, body, symphysis):** Cefazolin until fixation + 24 h post-op [38]
- **Lacerations:**
  - Simple (no tissue loss): **No antibiotics** [38]
  - Contaminated/over cartilage: Clindamycin or amoxicillin-clavulanate, ≤5 days [38]
- *Evidence: Traditional AAST guidance*

***

### **SLIDE 8: FACIAL FRACTURES – LATEST EVIDENCE (2024–2025)**
**SCOPING REVIEW KEY FINDING [38]:** Prolonging antibiotics >24 h for surgically treated facial fractures **provides no additional benefit** and increases resistance. [NOTE: I will expand this in MEDIUM/LONG]

| **Fracture Type** | **Prophylaxis Duration** | **Evidence Quality** | **Infection Rate** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mandible (dentate), operative** | Until fixation + 24 h post-op | Moderate | 8.9% with ≤24 h vs 5.7% with multi-day [38] |
| **Mid-face, operative** | Single preop dose sufficient | Moderate-High | 0% with single dose vs 0% with 24 h [38] |
| **Upper face, operative** | Single preop dose | High | No benefit to >24 h [38] |
| **Non-operative (any)** | **None** | High | No infections without antibiotics [38] |

- **Recommendation 2024:** Discontinue prophylaxis after 24 h for all operative facial fractures [38]
- **Special:** Basilar skull + CSF leak = ceftriaxone 2 g q12h × 5 days (until CSF stops) [38]
- *Evidence: NIH scoping review (2022) [38], UAB/WVU protocols (2024)*

***

### **SLIDE 9: Penetrating Injuries – Timing Barrier**
- **Challenge:** Penetrating abdominal/thoracic injuries may have pre-hospital delays [38]
- **Median time to antibiotics:** 69–90 minutes in penetrating trauma [38]
- **Why late?** Pre-hospital time, transport to OR, competing priorities [38]
- **Solution:** **Standardized protocols + multidisciplinary huddles reduce time** [31]
  - Example: Automated alerts in ED when penetrating trauma arrives [31]
  - Result: Time reduced from 69 min → 39 min (50% improvement) [31]
- **Institutional ** Facilities with <60 min to antibiotics = 8.5% SSI vs. 12% at >120 min [38]
- *Evidence: AAST 2024, quality improvement literature*

***

### **SLIDE 10: Special Populations**
| **Population** | **Modification** | **Evidence** |
|---|---|---|
| **Immunocompromised (CD4 <14% or HIV)** | Extend facial fracture antibiotics 7–10 days | Risk of delayed infection [38] |
| **Renal impairment (CrCl <30)** | Adjust aminoglycosides, reduce frequency | Nephrotoxicity risk [30] |
| **Pregnancy** | Avoid fluoroquinolones; use beta-lactams | Teratogenicity data [30] |
| **Beta-lactam allergy** | Use clindamycin 900 mg IV q8h | No cross-reactivity if not anaphylaxis [30] |
| **MRSA colonization** | Consider vancomycin for open fractures | Institutional prevalence guides [30] |

### **SLIDE 11: Common Mistakes & Pitfalls**
**MISTAKES:**
- ❌ **Late administration (>1 h):** Each hour delay → 7.6% ↑ infection rate [31]
- ❌ **Over-prescribing (>24 h):** Increases resistance, no benefit [38]
- ❌ **Wrong antibiotic:** Gentamicin for Grade I fractures (insufficient anaerobic coverage) [30]
- ❌ **Omitting antibiotics in non-operative fractures:** Unnecessary but harmless [38]

**CORRECT APPROACH:**
- ✅ **Within 1 hour** of injury/arrival [30][31]
- ✅ **≤24 hours** for most trauma [30]
- ✅ **Site-appropriate** (facial fractures vs open fractures vs penetrating CNS) [30][38]
- ✅ **Transition to IV→PO** when tolerating (reduce hospital stay) [30]

### **SLIDE 15: Open Fracture Classification & Antibiotic Specifics**
**Gustilo-Anderson Scale:**
- **Type I:** Wound <1 cm, minimal contamination
  - Antibiotic: **Cefazolin** 1 g IV
  - Duration: ≤24 h [30]
- **Type II:** Wound 1–10 cm, moderate contamination
  - Antibiotic: **Cefazolin + gentamicin** (e.g., 1 g + 5 mg/kg IV)
  - Duration: ≤24 h [30]
- **Type III:** Wound >10 cm, severe contamination, soil/marine
  - Antibiotic: **Cefazolin + gentamicin + clindamycin** (anaerobes)
  - Duration: ≤24 h [30]
  - **Special:** IIIb (vascular injury) or IIIc (vascular repair) = higher mortality, same antibiotic [30]
- **Dosing notes:** 
  - Gentamicin adjusted for renal function; consider level monitoring [30]
  - Clindamycin 900 mg IV q8h (good anaerobic coverage) [30]

***

### **SLIDE 16: Intra-Abdominal Contamination Management**
- **Indications:** Penetrating abdomen/flank, blunt with organ injury [30]
- **Antibiotic options:**
  - **Cefoxitin** 2 g IV q6h (covers Gram-neg + anaerobes) [30]
  - **Ampicillin-sulbactam** 3 g IV q6h [30]
  - **Piperacillin-tazobactam** 4.5 g IV q6–8h (if high-risk pathogens) [30]
- **Timing:** <1 hour [30]
- **Duration:**
  - **Prophylaxis only:** ≤24 h (if no contamination at operation) [30]
  - **Therapeutic:** If contamination found (bowel perforation), treat as complicated IAI (7–14 days) [30]
- **Culture:** Obtain intraop if possible (doesn't delay antibiotics) [30]
- *Evidence: AAST, surgical infection society*

### **SLIDE 17: Facial Fractures Deep Dive – Detailed Site-Specific**
**Upper Third (Frontal, Frontal Sinus):**
- **Non-involving posterior table:** Single preop cefazolin [38]
- **Involving posterior table:** Cefazolin <24 h post-op [38]
- **Rationale:** Lower infection risk; limited vascular supply [38]

**Mid-face (LeFort, ZMC, Orbital):**
- **Non-operative:** No antibiotics [38]
- **Operative:** Single preop dose (cefazolin) [38]
- **Infection rate (operative, single dose):** 0–3% (no benefit to prolongation) [38]
- **Note:** Mid-face has excellent vascular supply; rarely infected [38]

**Lower Third / Mandible (Most Complex):**
- **Condyle/Subcondylar (non-dentate):** Single preop dose [38]
- **Dentate (angle, body, symphysis, parasymphysis):**
  - Prophylaxis until fixation **+ 24 h post-op** [38]
  - Rationale: Proximity to teeth/saliva; higher infection risk [38]
  - Drug: Cefazolin OR ampicillin-sulbactam [38]
  - Duration: Should NOT exceed 48 h total (no benefit, resistance ↑) [38]

**Basilar Skull Fracture + CSF Leak:**
- Ceftriaxone 2 g IV q12h [38]
- Until CSF leak resolves OR 5 days (whichever first) [38]
- Higher pneumocephalus risk; CNS penetration essential [38]

***

### **SLIDE 18: Dental/Periodontal Considerations in Facial Trauma**
- **Teeth involvement:** Dictates antibiotic regimen [38]
- **Dentate segments:** Mandibular angle, body, parasymphysis, symphysis [38]
  - Flora: Mixed anaerobes + aerobes (gram-neg + gram-pos) [38]
  - Rationale for longer prophylaxis [38]
- **Non-dentate segments:** Condyle, subcondylar, ramus (edentulous or above tooth line) [38]
  - Lower infection risk; single dose sufficient [38]
- **Tooth loss in trauma:** Consider post-op antibiotic if infection risk (e.g., open socket) [38]

***

### **SLIDE 19: Allergy Considerations & Substitutions**
| **Allergy** | **Manifestation** | **Alternative** | **Notes** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PCN (mild rash, not anaphylaxis)** | Non-IgE mediated | Cefazolin OK (1–2% cross-reactivity) | Proceed with caution [30] |
| **PCN (anaphylaxis)** | IgE mediated | Clindamycin 900 mg IV q8h | No cross-reactivity [30] |
| **Cephalosporin** | Any severity | Clindamycin or fluoroquinolone | If non-PCN allergy (safe with cephalosporin) [30] |
| **Aminoglycoside (gentamicin)** | Ototoxicity/nephrotoxicity | Fluoroquinolone (if Gram-neg needed) | Monitor renal function [30] |

***

### **SLIDE 20: Antibiotic Stewardship & Resistance Prevention**
- **Problem:** 75% of trauma surgeons over-prescribe facial fracture antibiotics [38]
- **Consequence:** MRSA prevalence ↑ from 18% → 34% in trauma centers (12-year trend) [38]
- **Best practice:** **Discontinue after 24 h** (strong evidence) [38]
- **Communication:** ED/surgeon protocols clarifying 24-h rule [38]
- **Monitoring:** Track antibiotic duration per patient [38]
- **Outcome:** Reducing unnecessary prophylaxis → reduced resistance, same/better infection rates [38]
- *Evidence: Stewardship literature, trauma registry analysis*

### **SLIDE 21: Case Vignette #1 – Open Fracture Protocol**
**Scenario:** 28-year-old male, crush injury to leg, Gustilo Type II open femur fracture. Arrives 75 min post-injury.
- **Initial assessment:**
  - Wound: 3 cm laceration, visible bone, moderate contamination (dirt) [30]
  - Bleeding: Controlled with tourniquet [30]
- **Antibiotic protocol:**
  - Type II = Cefazolin 2 g IV + gentamicin 5 mg/kg IV [30]
  - Timing: 75 min (just over 1-hour target) – but still within therapeutic window [31]
- **Duration:** 24 h post-injury (then stop) [30]
- **OR timing:** Immediate (within 3 h of injury recommended for open fractures) [30]
- **Follow-up:** Cultures taken intraop; if no contamination found, continue prophylaxis only [30]
- *Evidence: AAST open fracture guidelines*

***

### **SLIDE 22: Case Vignette #2 – Facial Fracture Complexity**
**Scenario:** 45-year-old female, motor vehicle crash, mandibular fracture (parasymphyseal = dentate region), requires ORIF.
- **Traditional approach:** Many surgeons would give 3–5 days of antibiotics [38]
- **Updated approach (2024):** [38]
  - Preop: Cefazolin 2 g IV (~1 h before surgery) [38]
  - Postop: Continue cefazolin until midnight day of surgery [38]
  - Day 1 post-op: STOP (24-h total rule) [38]
- **Evidence basis:** [38]
  - Infection rate with 24 h = 8.9%
  - Infection rate with prolonged (3–5 days) = 5.7% (likely confounding, not causation) [38]
  - **No additional benefit to >24 h** [38]
- **Benefit:** Reduces resistance, cost, adverse events [38]
- *Evidence: NIH scoping review, AAST 2024*

***

### **SLIDE 23: Case Vignette #3 – Penetrating CNS**
**Scenario:** 32-year-old male, gunshot wound to head, frontal lobe injury, operative management planned.
- **Antibiotic choice:** NOT standard cefazolin (poor CNS penetration)
  - **Use:** Ceftriaxone 2 g IV q12h [30]
  - Why: Third-generation cephalosporin, high CSF penetration [30]
- **Duration:** ≤48 h (brief course, not prolonged) [30]
  - Prolonged antibiotics don't prevent infection; increase resistance [30]
- **Timing:** <1 h [30]
- **Monitoring:** Watch for meningitis (fever, nuchal rigidity, altered mentation) [30]
- **Day 3:** STOP ceftriaxone (prophylaxis period over) [30]
- *Evidence: AAST 2024 penetrating CNS guidelines*

## CHALK TALK (15–20 MIN, NO SLIDES)

### **Opening Case:**
"33-year-old male, pedestrian struck by car. Compound fracture to tibia/fibula with 4 cm laceration, dirt visible. ETA to OR: 85 minutes from injury. What's your antibiotic strategy?"

### **Teaching Block 1: Injury Assessment & Urgency**
- **Draw on whiteboard:**
  ```
  Compound fracture + visible dirt
      ↓
  Gustilo Type II (1–10 cm, moderate contamination)
      ↓
  Antibiotics: Cefazolin + gentamicin
  Timing: <1 hour (CRITICAL window)
  Duration: 24 hours (not extended)
  ```
- **Ask resident:** "Why is timing so critical?"
  - Answer: Each hour delay → ~7.6% ↑ infection rate [31]
  - By 85 minutes, patient is already at threshold; get antibiotics NOW [31]

### **Teaching Block 2: Antibiotic Selection**
- **Why cefazolin + gentamicin (not cefazolin alone)?** [30]
  - Cefazolin: Covers Gram-positive + some Gram-negative [30]
  - Gentamicin: Extended Gram-negative coverage (especially Pseudomonas) [30]
  - Combination robust against soil pathogens [30]
- **Ask resident:** "What if it was Gustilo Type I (small wound)?"
  - Answer: Cefazolin alone would suffice [30]
- **What about anaerobes?** Type II doesn't usually need clindamycin; Type III (soil) does [30]

### **Decision Point #1: How Long to Continue Antibiotics?**
- **Traditional thinking:** "Give antibiotics for several days"
  - WRONG – increases resistance, increases cost, no benefit [38]
- **Evidence-based:** ≤24 hours [30]
  - After 24 hours, prophylactic benefit plateau [30]
  - Continuing risks resistance without additional infection prevention [30]
- **Your patient:** Give antibiotics now (85 min post-injury), STOP at 24 h [30]
- **Ask resident:** "But what if he needs a second surgery?"
  - Answer: Restart prophylaxis only if >24 h from first dose [30]

### **Teaching Block 3: The 1-Hour Goal – Institutional Reality**
- **Ideal:** Antibiotics within 1 hour of injury [31]
- **Reality:** Current median = 69–90 minutes [31]
  - Where's the delay? Pre-hospital (45%), ED ordering (30%), pharmacy (15%) [31]
- **Solution at your institution:** Standing orders [31]
  - **Example:** When "penetrating trauma" called in, pharmacy auto-prepares antibiotics [31]
  - **Result:** 50% reduction in time (69 → 39 min) [31]
- **Your role:** Push for systemic improvements [31]

### **Decision Point #2: Facial Fracture – How Long?**
- **Separate scenario:** 45-year-old female, mandibular fracture (angle region = dentate)
- **Old guidance (pre-2024):** "Give 3–5 days of antibiotics" [38]
- **NEW evidence (2024):** [38]
  - Scoping review of 11 RCTs showed **no additional benefit to >24 h** [38]
  - 75% of surgeons still over-prescribe [38]
  - **Guidelines now say: 24 h max** (same 24-h rule as other trauma) [38]
- **Why the old guidance lingered?** [38]
  - Mandible adjacent to teeth (saliva flora); seemed logical to extend antibiotics [38]
  - But RCT data didn't support it [38]
- **Your approach:** Preop antibiotics + STOP at 24 h post-op [38]

### **Teaching Block 4: Special Situations**
- **Penetrating CNS (head):** Different antibiotic [30]
  - **NOT cefazolin** (poor CNS penetration) [30]
  - **USE ceftriaxone 2 g q12h** (third-gen cephalosporin, high CSF levels) [30]
  - **Duration:** Only 48 h (then stop) [30]
  - Why? CSF sterilization occurs by 48 h; longer antibiotics risk resistance [30]

- **Open mandible fracture (dentate):** Longer prophylaxis OK [38]
  - Cefazolin until fixation + 24 h post-op [38]
  - Rationale: Saliva/dental flora; higher infection risk [38]
  - **But still not indefinite – 48 h max usually** [38]

### **Teaching Block 5: Allergy Management**
- **Patient penicillin-allergic (anaphylaxis history)** [30]
  - Can't use cefazolin [30]
  - **Alternative:** Clindamycin 900 mg IV q8h [30]
  - Covers Gram-positive + anaerobes (good for trauma) [30]
- **Patient penicillin-allergic (mild rash, not anaphylaxis)** [30]
  - Can use cefazolin (only ~1–2% cross-reactivity) [30]
  - **Ask attending:** Proceed with caution [30]

### **Wrap-Up: Back to the Case**
"Your Type II open tibia/fibula, 85 minutes post-injury:
1. **Cefazolin 2 g IV + gentamicin 5 mg/kg IV** (Type II coverage)
2. **Give NOW** (already 85 min; still within window)
3. **Continue to 24 h** (then STOP)
4. **To OR** for irrigation, debridement, fixation
5. **Cultures** if contamination found intraop
6. **Monitor renal function** (gentamicin nephrotoxicity)"

**Ask residents:**
- "What if it was Gustilo Type III with soil?" → Add clindamycin [30]
- "What if patient allergic to penicillin?" → Clindamycin instead [30]
- "Facial fracture – how long?" → 24 h max (new 2024 evidence) [38]
- "Penetrating head wound – what antibiotic?" → Ceftriaxone, not cefazolin [30]
