## TRAUMA RESUSCITATION \& HEMORRHAGE CONTROL (Topics 1-6)

### 1. **xABCDE Protocol: The ATLS 11th Edition Paradigm Shift** [^1_1][^1_2][^1_3]

The most significant change in trauma education is the evolution from traditional ABCDE to **xABCDE**, where "x" represents immediate control of **exsanguinating hemorrhage** before traditional airway assessment. The algorithm emphasizes Shock Index and Pulse Pressure as key physiologic metrics. Permissive hypotension targets (SBP 80-100 mmHg) are now standard until major bleeding control is achieved. Special attention is given to age-stratified approaches and frailty assessment rather than chronological age alone.

### 2. **Massive Transfusion Protocols (MTP): 2024 Evidence Update** [^1_4][^1_5][^1_6][^1_7]

Early MTP activation is crucial—HMGB-1 levels correlate with trauma-induced coagulopathy and bleeding severity. Whole blood transfusion (when available) should be initiated immediately upon arrival for superior survival benefit; delays as brief as 14 minutes significantly reduce survival advantage. The **PROPPR trial** demonstrated 1:1:1 balanced transfusion (RBCs:FFP:Platelets) reduces 24-hour mortality from exsanguination by approximately 9% compared to component-based resuscitation.

### 3. **Damage Control Resuscitation (DCR) \& Damage Control Surgery (DCS)** [^1_8][^1_9][^1_10][^1_11][^1_12]

DCS is the standard for physiologically unstable trauma patients—survival improved from 11% to 77%. The "lethal triad" (hypothermia, acidosis, coagulopathy) drives decision-making. Recent meta-analysis shows **delayed reoperation beyond 48 hours reduces re-bleeding risk without increasing mortality or infection**—challenging traditional 24-48 hour dogma and supporting individualized patient physiology-based timing.

### 4. **Tranexamic Acid (TXA): Timing is Everything** [^1_9][^1_13][^1_14]

TXA is most effective within 1 hour of injury (32% mortality reduction) and retains benefit through 3 hours (21% reduction), with increased mortality risk if delayed beyond this window. The mechanism involves both antifibrinolytic and anti-inflammatory effects. Recent studies show IV bolus within 1 hour is superior to prolonged infusion protocols. TXA should be standard in all massive transfusion protocols.

### 5. **Hemorrhage Control: Pelvic Trauma Management** [^1_15][^1_16][^1_17]

Three competing strategies exist for pelvic hemorrhage control:

- **Pelvic Packing (PP)**: Direct access with 24.9% utilization
- **Angioembolization (AE)**: 21.4% utilization, superior outcomes with early timing
- **REBOA**: 7.6% utilization, controversial evidence in pelvic trauma

Recent evidence shows **early AE (timely angiography within 1-2 hours)** significantly reduces transfusion requirements, ICU LOS, and mortality compared to delayed intervention.

### 6. **Resuscitative Endovascular Balloon Occlusion of the Aorta (REBOA)** [^1_16][^1_18][^1_19]

REBOA serves as non-compressible torso hemorrhage (NCTH) control in Zone 1 (suprarenal) and Zone 3 (infrarenal) positioning. Median time to inflation is 7 minutes (IQR 5-11). Systolic BP increases by 40 mmHg on average. However, **subgroup analysis shows increased mortality in pelvic trauma (OR 2.15)**, while benefits emerge in traumatic cardiac arrest vs resuscitative thoracotomy (OR 0.32). Obstetric hemorrhage/PAS represents strongest evidence base.

## ORGAN-SPECIFIC TRAUMA MANAGEMENT (Topics 7-11)

### 7. **Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI): Age-Appropriate Management** [^1_20][^1_21][^1_22][^1_23]

**Permissive hypotension is absolutely contraindicated in TBI.** The Brain Trauma Foundation recommends CPP targets of 60-70 mmHg, but emerging evidence using **pressure reactivity index (PRx)** suggests individualized CPPopt is superior. Recent 2025 studies show asymmetric CPP/PRx relationships—decreases below CPPopt are more harmful than increases above it. Age-based MAP targets now recommended: 110 mmHg (15-49 yo), 100 mmHg (50-69 yo), 110 mmHg (≥70 yo).

### 8. **Acute Traumatic Coagulopathy (ATC) Management** [^1_24][^1_25][^1_13]

ATC involves rapid fibrinogen consumption, elevated thrombin generation, and pathologic fibrinolysis. **TXA normalizes ATC**, while rFVIIa causes paradoxical over-correction with increased thromboembolism and mortality (50% vs 80% survival in mouse models). Fibrinogen concentrate and prothrombin complex concentrate reduce transfusion volumes but **don't improve mortality**—they should be reserved for documented deficiencies on viscoelastic testing.

### 9. **Spinal Cord Injury (SCI): Critical Care Management** [^1_26][^1_27][^1_28]

Neurogenic shock occurs in 35% of SCI patients with injury ≥C5. Management priorities:

- **MAP target >85 mmHg** for first week post-injury
- First-line: aggressive fluid resuscitation with crystalloids
- Second-line: norepinephrine (cervical/thoracic) or phenylephrine (lower thoracic)
- Thromboprophylaxis: enoxaparin + mechanical prophylaxis for 3 months
- **Early decompression within 24 hours** improves neurologic recovery, reduces intubation time, and shortens ICU stay


### 10. **Crush Injury \& Rhabdomyolysis-Associated AKI** [^1_29][^1_30][^1_31][^1_32][^1_33]

Crush syndrome pathophysiology centers on myoglobin release, hyperkalemia, and ferroptosis-driven AKI. Management:

- **Early aggressive fluid resuscitation** (mainstay of treatment)
- Alkaline urine targets (sodium bicarbonate)
- Avoid early hypocalcemia correction (worsens calcium deposition)
- **RRT indications based on traditional AKI criteria**, not myoglobin levels
- Emerging therapies: antioxidants, hyperbaric oxygen, erythropoietin


### 11. **Burn Resuscitation: Formula Evolution \& Current Approach** [^1_34][^1_35][^1_36][^1_37][^1_38]

The **Modified Brooke formula (2 mL/kg/%TBSA)** is now preferred over Parkland (4 mL/kg/%TBSA) based on 2023 registry data—higher initial rates increase 24-hour fluid volumes by 15-28% without reducing complications. Adjunctive fresh frozen plasma (0.5 mL/kg/%TBSA/24 hours) is integrated into current protocols. Goal-directed resuscitation with **urine output targets (0.5 mL/kg/hour) is essential** to prevent under/over-resuscitation complications.

## EMERGENCY RESUSCITATIVE PROCEDURES (Topics 12-14)

### 12. **Emergency Resuscitative Thoracotomy (ERT)** [^1_39][^1_40][^1_41][^1_42][^1_43]

**2025 JAMA Surgery study** demonstrates prehospital ERT is feasible and improves survival in physician-led systems:

- **Cardiac tamponade**: 52.2% survival if TCA <1 minute; 25% if 5-10 minutes; 2.4% if >10 minutes
- Less effective for exsanguination-induced arrest
- **Cardiac motion on ultrasound** supports proceeding with procedure
- Absence of cardiac motion suggests futility
- Indications: penetrating thoracic trauma with cardiac arrest


### 13. **Resuscitative Hysterotomy (formerly Perimortem Cesarean Section)** [^1_44][^1_45][^1_46]

Resuscitative hysterotomy is performed to relieve aortocaval compression during maternal cardiac arrest:

- **Timing: within 5 minutes of collapse** (no survivors beyond 15 minutes)
- Maternal survival improved by removing fetal/placental obstruction
- **50% maternal survival** reported in recent case series
- Urgent post-delivery hemorrhage management required
- Emerging evidence supports maternal-centric approach with fetal benefits as secondary outcome


### 14. **Ruptured Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (rAAA)** [^1_47][^1_48][^1_49]

rAAA is a vascular emergency with 40-60% perioperative mortality even with intervention:

- **Rapid diagnosis (CT) → immediate OR** is critical
- Size thresholds: ≥5.5 cm men, ≥5.0 cm women warrant elective repair
- Open repair vs EVAR have similar long-term survival
- EVAR associated with lower early perioperative mortality but requires long-term surveillance
- **50% of rAAA patients reaching hospital alive; 50% of those don't survive repair**

## TRAUMA ANTIBIOTIC STEWARDSHIP (Topic 15)

### 15. **Antibiotic Prophylaxis in Trauma: Tailored Timing \& Duration** [^1_50][^1_51][^1_52][^1_53]

Evidence-based prophylaxis:

- **Timing within 1 hour of injury** (gold standard for all trauma types)
- **Duration ≤24 hours** for most injuries (open fractures, facial trauma)
- Penetrating CNS injury: short course only (≤3 days)
- **Multidisciplinary protocols reduce median time to antibiotics** from 69 to 39 minutes
- Penetrating injuries have 80-minute median vs 54-minute blunt trauma delays
- Overuse increases MDR pathogens and complications without benefit

## SURGICAL ICU CORE MANAGEMENT (Topics 16-23)

### 16. **Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP) Prevention: 2024 Evidence** [^1_54][^1_55][^1_56]

VAP incidence 2-16 per 1000 ventilator days (highly center-dependent). Evidence-based prevention:

- VAP care bundle compliance (69%) correlates with **1.2-day shorter LOS** and fewer complications
- **Oral care and secretion management** are foundational
- Nurses' compliance with bundle decreases over admission—requires ongoing reinforcement
- Multiplex PCR testing enables **antimicrobial de-escalation** in select centers
- VAP accounts for 50% of ICU antibiotics—stewardship critical


### 17. **ARDS Management: From One-Size-Fits-All to Precision Medicine** [^1_57][^1_58][^1_59]

Traditional ARDS management is shifting toward **phenotype-based precision approaches**:

- **Lung-protective ventilation** (tidal volume 6-8 mL/kg IBW, PEEP optimization)
- **Prone positioning** for moderate-severe ARDS (increases recruitment in exudative phase)
- **Fluid management** varies by phenotype: conservative for pulmonary ARDS; liberal for extrapulmonary
- **VV-ECMO** for refractory hypoxemia (64.7% survival vs 23.5% conventional ventilation)
- Emerging biomarkers (genomics, proteomics) guiding subphenotype-specific therapy


### 18. **Pain, Agitation, Sedation \& Delirium: 2025 SCCM PADIS Update** [^1_60][^1_61][^1_62][^1_63][^1_64]

**Major 2025 update** to SCCM guidelines emphasizes anxiety as new topic:

- **Dexmedetomidine over propofol** for light sedation (conditional; moderate certainty)
    - Benefits: reduced delirium, better titratability
    - Risk: bradycardia (manageable)
- **Benzodiazepines**: insufficient evidence for anxiety (no recommendation)
- **Antipsychotics**: unable to recommend vs placebo for delirium (low certainty)
- **CAM-ICU every 4 hours** for delirium assessment
- **Melatonin conditional recommendation** (low risk, potential sleep benefit)


### 19. **Sepsis Management in Surgical ICU: SSC Bundles 2024** [^1_65][^1_66][^1_67]

Surviving Sepsis Campaign framework:

- **Early recognition** (qSOFA, lactate >2)
- **Blood cultures before antibiotics** (don't delay treatment)
- **Antibiotics within 1 hour** (critical for mortality reduction)
- **Source control** as early as possible (procedural priority)
- **Lactate clearance** as resuscitation goal
- Vasopressor strategy: norepinephrine first-line
- New 2025 focus on **low-resource setting implementation**


### 20. **Delirium in Critical Illness: Prevention, Identification, Management** [^1_60][^1_61][^1_62]

Delirium affects 40-80% of trauma ICU patients:

- **Modifiable factors**: sedation depth, sleep, mobilization, noise, lights, family presence
- **CAM-ICU** (Confusion Assessment Method for ICU): validated assessment tool
- Pharmacologic management controversial—**antipsychotics not recommended routinely**
- **Non-pharmacologic bundle** (orientation, sleep hygiene, mobilization) most effective
- **Long-term cognitive impairment** (PICS) in 25-30% of delirium survivors


### 21. **Early Mobilization \& Rehabilitation in Trauma ICU** [^1_68][^1_69]

Major safety and outcomes evidence:

- **No adverse events** related to early mobilization protocols
- **Pneumonia reduction** (RR 0.79), **DVT reduction** (RR 0.67), **airway complications** (RR 0.52)
- Progressive mobility levels from passive ROM → active-assisted → ambulation
- **1.5-day shorter hospital LOS** (adjusted for severity)
- Each additional 10 minutes PT/OT = **1.2 days shorter hospital stay**


### 22. **Nutrition Support in Critical Illness \& Trauma** [^1_70][^1_71][^1_72][^1_73]

Evidence-based nutrition:

- **Early enteral nutrition** (within 24-48 hours post-resuscitation)
- **Hypocaloric feeding** (protein priority >calories): safe in trauma; reduces transfusion
- Protein targets: 1.2-2.0 g/kg/day (obesity-adjusted)
- **Refeeding syndrome prevention** (check BMP, PO4, Mg daily during advancement)
- High nutrition risk (malnutrition on admission, prolonged GI dysfunction): **initiate PN early**


### 23. **Post-Intensive Care Syndrome (PICS) \& Mental Health Screening** [^1_74][^1_75][^1_72][^1_76]

ICU survivors face substantial mental health burden:

- **PTSD**: 4-62% prevalence (varies by trauma type)
- **Anxiety/Depression**: 6/10 patients affected at 6-week follow-up; 4/10 at 6 months
- **Risk factors**: younger age, female sex, prolonged ICU stay, frightening memories
- **Screening timing**: within 1 month post-discharge (sensitive for delayed-onset symptoms)
- **Modifiable intervention**: reducing frightening memories improves long-term outcomes

## ADVANCED ICU \& SPECIALIZED TRAUMA TOPICS (Topics 24-28)

### 24. **Point-of-Care Ultrasound (POCUS) in Trauma \& ICU** [^1_77][^1_78][^1_79]

POCUS competency framework:

- **FAST/eFAST**: 25-50 supervised exams for core competency
- **Cardiac POCUS**: 50 FoCUS exams + 100 interpreted (ACC Level 1)
- **Lung ultrasound**: 30 Level 1 + 20 Level 2 exams
- **Image quality standardization** essential (only 20% of trainee FAST exams diagnostic quality across all windows)
- **Procedural guidance** for central lines, thoracostomy, paracentesis


### 25. **Extracorporeal Membrane Oxygenation (ECMO) in Trauma** [^1_80][^1_81]

ECMO represents salvage therapy for refractory cardiopulmonary failure:

- **VV-ECMO for ARDS**: 64% pooled survival (vs 23.5% conventional ventilation)
- **Polytrauma with ARDS**: 64% survival
- **TBI with ARDS**: 67% survival
- **VA-ECMO for cardiogenic shock/cardiac arrest**: emerging evidence
- **Anticoagulation management** and **bleeding complications** are primary concerns
- Patient selection criteria and **early initiation** critical for success


### 26. **Geriatric Trauma: Age is Not the Problem—Frailty Is** [^1_82][^1_83][^1_84][^1_85]

Paradigm shift from age-based to frailty-based assessment:

- **Trauma-Specific Frailty Index (15-variable TSFI)** superior to ISS/RTS/GCS for outcomes
- Frail patients: more sepsis (1.5×), hematologic complications, pulmonary complications
- Falls cause 91% of geriatric trauma (vs younger populations with intentional injury)
- **Early frailty identification** guides disposition and risk stratification
- Long-term outcomes worse in frail (mortality, complications, discharge disposition)


### 27. **Pediatric Trauma: Age-Specific Physiologic Parameters** [^1_86][^1_87][^1_88][^1_89]

Pediatric-specific approaches:

- **Peds-TRISS model** treats age as continuous variable (superior calibration to original TRISS)
- Age-adjusted vital signs essential (no adult cutoffs)
- **Mechanism differences**: falls in young children, pedestrian/cyclist in school age, MVA in adolescents
- **Imaging decision rules reduce CT scans** without compromising safety (PECARN criteria)
- Multidisciplinary pediatric teams with pediatric surgery, anesthesia, nursing expertise


### 28. **Blast Injury Management: Complex Polytrauma** [^1_90][^1_91][^1_92][^1_93]

Blast injuries involve multiple injury mechanisms:

- **Primary** (pressure wave): pulmonary barotrauma, tympanic rupture
- **Secondary** (projectiles): penetrating trauma
- **Tertiary** (displacement): blunt trauma and falls
- **Quaternary** (other): burns, inhalation injury
- **Blast-induced TBI** is "hidden wound" with high fatality
- **Damage control resuscitation** requires vigilant hemodynamic/ICP monitoring to balance CPP without worsening organ edema

- **Contraindications:** Active DIC (check PT/aPTT/fibrinogen before giving) [^4_16][^4_17]
- **Perioperative use:** Give **before incision** for blunt/penetrating trauma [^4_19][^4_16]
- **Second line:** If hemorrhage continues despite DCR + TXA, consider rFVIIa (controversial; monitor for thromboembolism) [^4_20][^4_16]
- *Evidence: ATLS 11, EAST 2024, trauma resuscitation guidelines*

***

### **SLIDE 12: Acute Traumatic Coagulopathy (ATC)**

- **ATC = trauma-induced coagulopathy from:**
    - Fibrinolysis (tissue plasminogen activator release) [^4_21]
    - Factor consumption (shock-driven activation) [^4_21]
    - Hypothermia + acidosis (slowed enzyme kinetics) [^4_21]
- **TXA mechanism:** Inhibits tissue plasminogen activator → stabilizes clot [^4_16][^4_17]
- **Point-of-care testing:** TEG/ROTEM guides fibrinogen vs. factor concentrate therapy [^4_21]
- *Evidence: ATC pathophysiology literature, viscoelastic testing studies*

***

### **SLIDE 13: Special Populations: Age \& Frailty Adjustments**

- **Age >55 with trauma:** Consider frailty assessment (not just ISS) [^4_22][^4_23]
- **Geriatric trauma:** Same xABCDE protocol but:
    - Maintain higher MAP (110 mmHg) even in early resuscitation [^4_8]
    - Higher transfusion requirements (polypharmacy, comorbidities) [^4_23]
    - Earlier ICU admission threshold [^4_23]
- **Pediatric:** Age-adjusted vitals essential; Shock Index same [^4_24]
- *Evidence: ACS geriatric trauma guidelines, pediatric TRISS*

***

### **SLIDE 14: Clinical Pearls \& Pitfalls**

**DO:**

- ✅ Activate MTP early (SI >1.2) before profound shock develops [^4_5]
- ✅ Give TXA within 1 hour of injury (not just in OR) [^4_16]
- ✅ Use permissive hypotension EXCEPT in TBI [^4_6][^4_8]
- ✅ Reassess physiology before planned re-op (individualize timing) [^4_14]

**DON'T:**

- ❌ Over-resuscitate with crystalloids alone (dilutional coagulopathy) [^4_10]
- ❌ Give late TXA (>3 hours) – increases mortality [^4_16]
- ❌ Delay DCS while pursuing "perfect" resuscitation [^4_13]

- **rFVIIa (Novo7):** Concern for thromboembolism

### **SLIDE 20: Temperature Management \& Lethal Triad Prevention**

- **Hypothermia (<32°C) indicator of DCS need** [^4_10][^4_13]

    - Minimize crystalloid (cold infusions cause iatrogenic hypothermia) [^4_10]
    - Rapid transport (avoid prolonged scene times) [^4_2]
    - Warm blankets, fluid warmers [^4_10]
- **Acidosis reversal:**
    - Tissue perfusion (resuscitation + hemorrhage control) [^4_10]
    - Monitor base deficit (goal: >–6 before re-op) [^4_14]
- *Evidence: DCS physiology, EAST guidelines*

### **SLIDE 21: Transition from DCR to Definitive Care**

- **When physiology improves:**
    - Core temp >35°C, pH >7.30, Base deficit >–6, Fibrinogen >100 mg/dL [^4_10][^4_14]
    - Intact coagulation (INR <1.5, PTT normal, platelet >50) [^4_10]
- **Planned re-operation (48–72 hours later):**
    - Allows normalization of coagulopathy [^4_14]
    - **Reduces re-bleeding vs. early re-op** [^4_14]
- **ICU management post-DCS:**
    - Ongoing monitoring, antibiotics, nutrition, mobilization [^4_28][^4_29]
- *Evidence: EAST 2024, recent re-op timing studies*

### **Teaching Block 1: What Changed with ATLS 11?**

- **Whiteboard draw:** Old ABCDE vs new xABCDE
- **Ask resident:** "What if he has active bleeding—do we intubate first?"
- **Answer:** If exsanguinating hemorrhage present, control that FIRST (direct pressure, tourniquet, OR)
- **Key concept:** xABCDE = hemorrhage control before airway (if life-threatening bleed)


### **Teaching Block 2: Shock Index in Real Time**

- **Whiteboard draw:** SI = HR/SBP
    - Calculate: 132 ÷ 78 = 1.69 → **Severe hemorrhage**
- **Ask:** "What do we do?" → **Activate MTP**
- **Why?** SI >1.2 predicts massive transfusion need, mortality [^4_5]
- **Pulse Pressure:** 78 – (difficult to feel diastolic) → narrow → confirms shock


### **Teaching Block 3: Permissive Hypotension—But Not Always**

- **Scenario A:** Stab wound, no head injury → Permissive SBP 80–100 mmHg ✅
- **Scenario B:** Stab wound + GCS 8 (brain injury) → Maintain SBP >90 mmHg ❌
- **Why?** TBI needs perfusion (CPP 60–70 mmHg). Permissive HoTn worsens brain injury [^4_8][^4_9]
- **Ask resident:** "How do you know if there's a head injury?" → Neuro exam, CT brain


### **Decision Point \#1: When to Go to OR?**

- **If SI persistent after 2L crystalloid + 2 units RBC** → **Go to OR for bleeding control**
- **DCS vs. definitive repair?** Depends on physiology:
    - Warm, normal pH, coagulation intact → Consider definitive repair [^4_10]
    - Cool, acidotic, coagulopathic → DCS now, re-op later [^4_10][^4_13]


### **Teaching Block 4: Tranexamic Acid Timing**

- **Whiteboard draw timeline:**
    - **0–1 hour post-injury:** TXA gives 32% mortality reduction [^4_16]
    - **1–3 hours:** Still beneficial (21% reduction) [^4_16]
    - **>3 hours:** HARMFUL (increased thrombosis, mortality ↑) [^4_16]
- **Mechanism:** Blocks fibrin breakdown (prevents fibrinolysis) [^4_16][^4_17]
- **Dose:** 1 g IV bolus + 1 g infusion over 8 hours [^4_16]


### **Decision Point \#2: Factor Concentrates or FFP?**

- **Scenario:** Coagulopathy on TEG (fibrinogen 80 mg/dL, PT 16)
- **Ask resident:** "Fibrinogen or FFP?"
    - **Answer:** Fibrinogen concentrate faster BUT **no mortality benefit** [^4_21][^4_20]
    - Reserve for **documented low fib** [^4_21]
    - FFP works but slower [^4_21]
- **rFVIIa?** NO – increased thromboembolism, worse outcomes [^4_20]


### **Teaching Block 5: Planned Re-operation Timing**

- **Old dogma:** Re-op in 24–48 hours
- **New evidence (2025):** Delayed re-op (>48 h) **reduces re-bleeding without ↑ mortality** [^4_14]
- **Whiteboard draw:** Two groups
    - **Early re-op:** More re-bleeding (12.4%)
    - **Delayed re-op:** Less re-bleeding (8.9%), same mortality [^4_14]
- **Implication:** Let physiology recover (warm, coagulation normalized) before re-op [^4_14]


### **Wrap-Up: Back to the Case**

"Your 32-year-old with GSW abdomen—SBP 78, SI 1.69, GCS 15:

1. **Activate MTP** (SI >1.2)
2. **Give TXA <1 hour** of injury
3. **Permissive hypotension** OK (no head injury, SBP 80–100 target)
4. **To OR** for hemorrhage control (DCS if unstable physiology)
5. **Plan re-op** based on recovery (not fixed 24–48 h)"

**Ask residents:**

- "If this patient had a GCS 7, what would change?" → Higher SBP target, ICU CPP targets
- "What if it's now 4 hours post-injury and he's acidotic?" → Consider DCS, but no late TXA

## SHORT VERSION (12 SLIDES, 15–20 MIN)

### **SLIDE 1: Title Slide**

- **Title:** Spinal Cord Injury: Neurogenic Shock, Critical Care, \& Recovery
- **Subtitle:** From Field to ICU – Evidence-Based Management 2024–2025
- **Setting:** Level 1 Trauma Center

***

### **SLIDE 2: SCI Epidemiology \& Pathophysiology**

- **Incidence:** ~54 cases per million per year (US) [^4_32][^4_33]
- **Mechanism:** Motor vehicle crashes (35%), falls (28%), violence (24%), sports (8%) [^4_34]
- **Neurogenic shock** (35% of SCI patients):
    - Occurs in **cervical/upper thoracic SCI** (T5 and above) [^4_33][^4_34]
    - Loss of sympathetic tone → bradycardia + hypotension [^4_33]
    - Vasodilation without tachycardia (unlike hypovolemic shock) [^4_33]
- **Window of opportunity:** Early intervention (within 24 h) may improve outcomes [^4_34]
- *Evidence: ACS/ENLS/Neurocritical Care Society guidelines*

***

### **SLIDE 3: Acute Neurogenic Shock: Recognition**

| **Finding** | **Typical Shock** | **Neurogenic Shock** |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **HR** | ↑ (tachycardia) | ↓ (bradycardia) or normal |
| **BP** | ↓ (hypotension) | ↓ (hypotension) |
| **Skin** | Pale, clammy | Warm, dry |
| **Cap refill** | Delayed | Normal |
| **Mechanism** | Hypovolemia | Vasodilation (↓ SVR) |

- **Key distinction:** Bradycardia + hypotension = neurogenic shock until proven otherwise [^4_33][^4_34]
- **Mimics:** Spinal shock (flaccid paralysis below injury) ≠ neurogenic shock [^4_33]
- *Evidence: Neurocritical care physiology*

***

### **SLIDE 4: MAP Targets in SCI: First Week is Critical**

- **Goal:** Maintain **MAP >85 mmHg** for first 7 days post-injury [^4_32][^4_33][^4_34]
    - Higher MAP improves spinal cord perfusion [^4_32]
    - Reduces secondary ischemic injury [^4_33]
    - **RCT evidence:** MAP augmentation → improved neurologic recovery [^4_35]
- **Age adjustments:** No current age-based modification (unlike TBI) [^4_34]
- **Monitoring:** Continuous arterial line (consider early given neurogenic shock risk) [^4_32]
- *Evidence: ACS Spine Guidelines, ENLS Protocol v5.0, recent RCTs*

***

### **SLIDE 5: Vasopressor Selection in SCI**

| **Drug** | **Injury Level** | **Mechanism** | **First-Line?** |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Phenylephrine** | Cervical/upper thoracic | Pure vasoconstriction | NO (↓ CO) |
| **Norepinephrine** | Cervical | Vaso + inotropic | YES ✓ |
| **Dopamine** | Upper thoracic (T1-T5) | Dose-dependent (see below) | Maybe |

- **Dopamine dosing:**
    - Low (2–4 μg/kg/min): Renal/splanchnic vasodilation
    - Medium (5–10 μg/kg/min): **Inotropic + mild vaso** → Often used in SCI [^4_34]
    - High (>10 μg/kg/min): Vasoconstriction (avoid)
- **Best practice:** Start **norepinephrine** if MAP <85 mmHg despite fluid [^4_32][^4_33]
- *Evidence: ENLS Protocol, critical care physiology*

***

### **SLIDE 6: Fluid Resuscitation in SCI: Balance**

- **Initial:** Aggressive crystalloid resuscitation (hemorrhage control is priority) [^4_32][^4_34]
- **Ongoing (post-hemorrhage control):** **Judicious fluid** (avoid pulmonary edema) [^4_32]
    - SCI → neurogenic shock (vasodilation, not hypovolemia)
    - Excess fluid doesn't improve MAP; vasopressors do [^4_32][^4_34]
- **Target:** Euvolemia (JVD, lung auscultation, UOP 0.5–1 mL/kg/h) [^4_32]
- **CVP/PCWP monitoring:** Consider if hypotension refractory to fluids + vasopressors [^4_32]
- *Evidence: ENLS, critical care guidelines*

***

### **SLIDE 7: Early Spinal Decompression**

- **Timing:** **Within 24 hours of injury** vs delayed (>24 h) [^4_34][^4_35]
- **Evidence:** Decompression within 24 h associated with:
    - Improved neurologic recovery (1–2 ASIA grade improvement) [^4_34]
    - Shorter ICU stay [^4_34]
    - Reduced respiratory complications [^4_34]
    - **RCT:** 65% favorable outcome (within 24h) vs 52% (>24h) [^4_35]
- **Operative approach:** Depends on injury anatomy (anterior, posterior, or both) [^4_34]
- **Risk:** Anesthesia + operative time in unstable patient [^4_34]
- *Evidence: ASIA Clinical Practice Guidelines, neurosurgery literature*

***

### **SLIDE 8: Respiratory Management in SCI**

- **High cervical (C1–C4):** Complete respiratory failure likely → mechanical ventilation [^4_32][^4_33]
- **Mid-cervical (C5–C8):** Variable; may require intubation for secretion mgmt [^4_32]
- **Thoracic (T1–T5):** Often preserve intercostal innervation; lower risk [^4_32]
- **Key:** Early tracheostomy (7–10 days) if prolonged ventilation anticipated [^4_32]
    - Facilitates weaning, reduces VAP risk [^4_32]
- **Abdominal binder:** Improves diaphragmatic mechanics in thoracic SCI [^4_32]
- *Evidence: ENLS Protocol, respiratory physiology*

***

### **SLIDE 9: Thromboprophylaxis in SCI**

- **Risk:** Immobilization + injury → VTE incidence 60–80% (untreated) [^4_32][^4_33]
- **Prophylaxis approach:**
    - **Mechanical:** Sequential compression devices (SCDs) + early mobilization [^4_32]
    - **Pharmacologic:** **Enoxaparin** (preferred) started within 24 hours of injury [^4_32][^4_33]
        - Dose: 30 mg SC BID (adjust for renal function)
        - Duration: **≥3 months** (long duration of risk) [^4_33]
    - **Inferior vena cava filter:** Only if contraindication to anticoagulation [^4_33]
- **Monitoring:** Duplex ultrasound if symptomatic [^4_32]
- *Evidence: ACCP guidelines, SCI thromboprophylaxis trials*

***

### **SLIDE 10: Long-Term Outcomes \& Rehabilitation**

- **Neurologic recovery:** ~50% of motor improvement occurs in first 3 months [^4_34][^4_36]
- **Plateau:** Most recovery complete by 1 year [^4_34]
- **Functional outcomes:**
    - Incomplete SCI: Better prognosis (50–60% walk independently) [^4_34]
    - Complete SCI: Limited ambulation (20–30% walk with assist) [^4_36]
- **Rehabilitation:** Aggressive PT/OT from day 1 → improved outcomes [^4_34]
- **Psychosocial:** PTSD, depression common; early screening recommended [^4_34]
- *Evidence: SCI rehabilitation trials, long-term follow-up studies*

***

### **SLIDE 11: Clinical Pearls \& Pitfalls**

**DO:**

- ✅ Maintain **MAP >85 mmHg** for 7 days post-injury (key intervention) [^4_32][^4_33]
- ✅ Use **norepinephrine** if fluid-refractory hypotension [^4_32]
- ✅ Pursue **early decompression within 24 h** if operatively feasible [^4_34][^4_35]
- ✅ Initiate **thromboprophylaxis** within 24 hours [^4_33]

**DON'T:**

- ❌ Use phenylephrine alone in SCI (decreases CO, worsens perfusion) [^4_32]

### **SLIDE 19: Detailed MAP Augmentation Strategy**

### **Opening Case:**

### **Teaching Block 1: Recognize Neurogenic Shock**

- **Draw on whiteboard:**

```
C5 SCI
    ↓
Loss of sympathetic tone (injury at C5)
    ↓
Vasodilation + No tachycardia response
    ↓
Bradycardia (vagal tone unopposed) + Hypotension
= NEUROGENIC SHOCK
```

- **Ask resident:** "What's different from hemorrhagic shock?"
    - Answer: **Bradycardia** (not tachycardia) + warm skin (not pale/clammy) [^4_33]


### **Teaching Block 2: Immediate Management**

- **What do you do?**

1. **Aggressive fluid** (1–2L crystalloid rapid) [^4_32]
2. **Central line** (monitor MAP) [^4_32]
3. **Vasopressor if MAP stays <85 mmHg** → **Norepinephrine** [^4_32][^4_33]
- **Why norepinephrine?** Combination of vasoconstriction + inotropic effect (better than pure vaso) [^4_32]
- **Timeline:** This is a **time-sensitive neurosurgical emergency** [^4_35]
    - **Early decompression (within 24 h) improves neurologic recovery** [^4_34][^4_35]


### **Decision Point \#1: Intubate Now or Wait?**

- **Consider respiratory status:**
    - **C5:** Partial phrenic innervation; may have adequate diaphragm function [^4_32]
    - **C1–C4:** Complete respiratory failure likely → intubate early [^4_32]
    - **This patient (C5):** Monitor vital capacity; intubate if declining [^4_32]
- **If intubating:** Early tracheostomy (day 7–10) if long-term vent likely [^4_32]


### **Teaching Block 3: MAP >85 mmHg for First 7 Days**

- **Why 85?** Not 65 like other shock [^4_32][^4_33]
    - SCI → swelling compresses microcirculation [^4_32]
    - Higher MAP "pushes through" the edema, improves blood flow [^4_32]
    - Evidence: RCT shows fewer intubation days with MAP >85 [^4_35]
- **Ask resident:** "How long do we maintain this?" → **7 days** [^4_32][^4_33][^4_35]
    - After 7 days, goal shifts to standard MAP ≥65 [^4_32]


### **Decision Point \#2: Emergency Decompression or Stabilization First?**

- **Scenario:** C5 fracture, BP stable on pressors, but radiology shows cord compression [^4_34][^4_35]
- **Ask resident:** "Do we go to OR now or wait?"
    - **Answer:** **Within 24 hours** is supported by RCT evidence [^4_34][^4_35]
    - Early decompression → better neuro recovery (1–2 ASIA grades better) [^4_34]
    - **Yes, proceed with early OR** (if medically stable enough) [^4_35]


### **Teaching Block 4: Thromboprophylaxis**

- **Risk in SCI:** 60–80% develop DVT/PE if untreated [^4_33]
- **Start:** Within 24 hours of injury [^4_33]
- **Drug:** Enoxaparin 30 mg SC BID [^4_33]
- **Duration:** **3 months** (much longer than other trauma) [^4_33]
- **Monitoring:** Duplex ultrasound if symptomatic [^4_33]


### **Teaching Block 5: Long-Term Outlook**

- **Ask resident:** "What's his prognosis at 1 year?"
    - **Depends on ASIA grade** (assessed at 24–72 hours) [^4_36]
    - **C5, motor incomplete (partially preserved)** → **60–80% chance of ambulation** [^4_34][^4_36]
    - **C5, motor complete** → Limited ambulation (assisted only) [^4_36]
- **Recovery timeline:** 50% improvement in first 3 months, plateau by 1 year [^4_34]

"Your C5 SCI patient—BP 88, HR 44:

1. **Aggressive fluids** (neurogenic shock needs volume)
2. **MAP >85 mmHg** (7-day critical window for spinal cord perfusion)
3. **Norepinephrine** if fluid-refractory
4. **Neurosurgery STAT** (early decompression within 24h improves outcomes)

### **SLIDE 2: Key Principle: Timing is Everything**

- **Golden rule:** Antibiotics **within 1 hour of injury** (or within 1 hour of arrival if delayed presentation) [^4_30][^4_31][^4_38]
- **Rationale:** Reduces surgical site infection (SSI) by ~30% [^4_30][^4_31]
- **Multiinstitutional data:** When 1-hour goal met, infection rates drop from 12% → 8.5% [^4_31][^4_38]
- **Reality check:** Current median time to antibiotics = 69–90 minutes (above 1-hour goal) [^4_31]
- **Barrier:** Prolonged pre-hospital times in penetrating injuries delay administration [^4_38]
- *Evidence: AAST 2024, EAST 2024, surgical infection literature*

***

### **SLIDE 3: Duration Rules: Less is More**

- **Standard trauma (most injuries):** ≤24 hours prophylaxis (no post-op antibiotics after 24 h) [^4_30][^4_31][^4_38]
- **Extended (select injuries):**
    - **Penetrating CNS injury:** Brief course only (24–48 h) [^4_30]
    - **Intra-abdominal contamination (iatrogenic):** 24 h post-op [^4_30]
    - **Facial fractures (dentate mandible):** Until fixation + 24 h post-op [^4_38]
- **Non-operative injuries:** **NO prophylactic antibiotics** (close fractures, simple lacerations) [^4_38]
- **Pitfall:** 75% of surgeons over-prescribe (continue >24 h unnecessarily) [^4_38]
- *Evidence: AAST guidelines, scoping reviews, antibiotic stewardship*

***

### **SLIDE 4: Antibiotics for Open Fractures: AAST Algorithm**

| **Open Fracture Grade** | **Antibiotic Regimen** | **Duration** | **Start** |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **I (minimal contamination)** | First-generation cephalosporin | ≤24 h | <1 h |
| **II (moderate contamination)** | 1st-gen cephalosporin + gentamicin | ≤24 h | <1 h |
| **III (severe/soil/marine)** | 1st-gen ceph + gentamicin **+ clindamycin** (anaerobes) | ≤24 h | <1 h |
| **III with vascular injury** | Same + operative urgency | ≤24 h | <1 h |

- **Dosing:** Cefazolin 1–2 g IV (2 g if >120 kg) [^4_30][^4_38]
- **Renal dosing:** Adjust gentamicin for CrCl [^4_30]
- *Evidence: AAST 2024, Gustilo \& Anderson classification*

### **SLIDE 5: Penetrating Abdominal Trauma**

- **Indication:** All penetrating abdominal/flank wounds [^4_30]
- **Antibiotic choice:** Cefoxitin (covers Gram-neg + anaerobes) OR Ampicillin-sulbactam [^4_30]
- **Duration:** **≤24 hours** if no therapeutic laparotomy findings [^4_30]
    - If contamination found (bowel perforation): Treat as complicated intra-abdominal infection (longer course) [^4_30]
- **Timing:** Within 1 hour [^4_30]
- **Update 2024:** No difference in outcomes between 24 h vs 48 h prophylaxis [^4_30]
- *Evidence: AAST 2024 trauma guidelines*

### **SLIDE 6: Penetrating CNS Injury (Head/Spine)**

- **Indication:** All penetrating head/spine injuries [^4_30]
- **Concern:** Meningitis risk (CSF leak, foreign body) [^4_30]
- **Antibiotic choice:** **Ceftriaxone 2 g IV q12h** (better CNS penetration than cefazolin) [^4_30][^4_38]
- **Duration:** **Brief course only (24–48 h)** then stop [^4_30]

### **SLIDE 15: Open Fracture Classification \& Antibiotic Specifics**
