## ANTIBIOTIC PROPHYLAXIS IN TRAUMA WITH EMPHASIS ON TRAUMATIC FACIAL FRACTURES: COMPREHENSIVE EVIDENCE SYNTHESIS \& TEACHING PACKAGE

### EXECUTIVE SUMMARY FOR LEVEL 1 TRAUMA CENTER

This synthesis provides evidence-based guidance for antibiotic prophylaxis across trauma injuries, with detailed recommendations for traumatic facial fractures—a common injury pattern where current practice often exceeds guideline recommendations. The 2024 AAST Critical Care Committee and 2021 Surgical Infection Society guidelines recommend **≤24 hours of perioperative prophylaxis** for open/contaminated facial fractures and **no prophylactic antibiotics** for closed/non-operative fractures, based on GRADE-rated evidence. Recent meta-analyses (2024–2025) show prolonged therapy provides no additional benefit and may increase infection risk, supporting an antibiotic stewardship approach.

***

### 1. EPIDEMIOLOGY \& BURDEN OF DISEASE

**Global Burden**[^2_1][^2_2]

- Annual incidence: 7.5 million facial fractures (95% UI 6.1–9.5 million, 2017)
- Age-standardized incidence: 98 (80–123) per 100,000 population
- Prevalence: 23 (20–27) per 100,000
- Years lived with disability (YLDs): 2 (1–2) per 100,000

**United States Epidemiology**[^2_3][^2_4]

- Incidence: 40.57 per 100,000 person-years
- Gender: Males 55.26% (IR 45.47), females 44.74% (IR 35.1); IRR 1.27 (M:F)
- Age peak: 80+ years (IR 186.57 per 100,000); second peak ages 20–29
- Incidence increasing in sports-related injuries; stable overall since 1990

**Fracture Distribution**[^2_5]

- Mandibular: 35% (highest risk for infection)
- Zygomatic: 24%
- Nasal: 23%
- Multiple facial fractures: 27.6% of patients

**Etiology**[^2_6][^2_5]

- Motor vehicle accidents: 24–70% (varies by region; RTAs highest in India, 70%)
- Assault/violence: 22–41%
- Falls: Primary cause globally (most regions)
- Sports: 9% civilian; increasing in youth
- Age: Peak ages 20–40 years; males 85%

**Mortality Risk by Location**[^2_7]

- Upper face fractures: 18.8% cumulative mortality
- Middle face fractures: 6.9% cumulative mortality
- Lower face fractures: 4.0% cumulative mortality
- Upper face associated with 4.06-fold increased risk of death and 4–6 fold risk of occult intracranial injury (even with normal GCS)

### 2. PATHOPHYSIOLOGY \& MECHANISM OF INJURY

**Soft Tissue Trauma Progression**

**Phase 1: Immediate (0–6 hours)**

- Laceration and fracture of bone
- Exposure of cancellous bone and marrow (high bacterial affinity)
- Blood clot formation; initial hemostasis
- Inflammatory cascade: IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α (peak 3–4 hours)
- Bacterial inoculation occurs (skin flora, environmental, oral flora if mandibular)

**Phase 2: Early Inflammatory (6–24 hours)**

- Neutrophil infiltration (max by 12–24 hours)
- Biofilm formation begins on fracture site and devitalized tissue
- If mandibular or oral exposure: oral flora (*Streptococcus*, *Prevotella*, anaerobes) colonize wound
- PMN migration peaks; opsonization by complement and antibodies
- Prophylactic antibiotics effective during this window (pre/perioperative)

**Phase 3: Progressive Infection (24 hours–2 weeks)**

- Biofilm maturation on bone and hardware (if ORIF)
- Transition from planktonic to sessile bacteria
- Increased antibiotic tolerance (ESBLs, altered metabolism)
- Fibroblast proliferation; extracellular matrix deposition begins
- Delay in surgery >7 days: Infection rate rises from 4.2% to 55%[^2_8]
- Osteoclast activation (RANKL/RANK); bone resorption begins

**Phase 4: Late Complications (>2 weeks)**

- Osteomyelitis: Sequestrum formation, cortical sclerosis
- Non-union: Disrupted callus formation (fracture healing inhibited)
- Chronic suppuration; sinus tract formation
- Fistula development (often intraoral-to-extraoral communication)

**Fracture-Specific Pathophysiology**

**Mandibular Fractures (Open/Contaminated)**

- Proximity to oral cavity (dentate regions: angle, body, symphysis, parasymphysis)
- 50% infection rate historically without prophylaxis (Chole 1987 control group: 43.9%)
- Oral anaerobes: *Peptostreptococcus*, *Prevotella*, *Porphyromonas*, *Fusobacterium*
- Mandibular condyle (non-dentate): Lower risk (not always considered open)
- Open reduction/fixation: 4-fold higher infection than closed reduction-MMF[^2_9]

**Midfacial/Upper Facial Fractures (Non-Contaminated)**

- Minimal oral flora exposure unless intraoral laceration present
- Le Fort fractures: Contact with nasal/sinus mucosa (lower risk than oral)
- Zygomatic (malar complex): Primarily skin flora risk
- Excellent blood supply: Better immune response, lower baseline infection (0–3.3% without prophylaxis)

**Posterior Table Frontal Sinus Fractures**

- CSF communication risk; intracranial space exposure
- CNS pathogen seeding possible (though rare: ~1% meningitis even without prophylaxis)
- Dural tear risk with violation >25% of posterior table
- Organisms: Pneumococcus, *H. influenzae*, oral anaerobes (if fracture tract to pharynx)

**Basilar Skull Fractures with CSF Leak**

- Cochrane meta-analysis: No difference in meningitis incidence with/without prophylactic antibiotics[^2_10]
- Meningitis rate: ~10% with CSF leak regardless of antibiotic use
- Prophylaxis not recommended; focus on fluid isolation and early repair

### 4. DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH \& DECISION POINTS AT LEVEL 1 TRAUMA CENTER

**Initial Assessment Protocol**

**Triage (ED Arrival)**

1. **ABCDE assessment**: Secure airway first (facial swelling/blood obstruction risk)
2. **Identify mechanism**:
    - Blunt (fall, MVA, assault): Closed fracture likely
    - Penetrating (GSW, stab, crush): Open/contaminated, contamination type unknown
    - Bite wound: Specific prophylaxis (mammalian)
3. **Assess contamination**:
    - Clean (low-energy, minimal tissue injury): Closed fracture
    - Contaminated (visible dirt, farm exposure, intraoral communication): High-risk open fracture
    - Dirty (fecal, soil, gunshot): Requires extended prophylaxis consideration

**Imaging at Arrival**

- **CT face (axial/coronal)**:
    - Fracture location (anterior vs. posterior mandible table)
    - Communication to oral cavity (mandible angle/body: presumed open)
    - Frontal sinus fracture type (anterior vs. posterior table)
    - CSF leak indicators (pneumocephalus, basilar fracture)
- **Head CT** (if GCS <15 or upper face fracture): Rule out TBI (4–6x higher risk with upper face)

**Microbiology Considerations**


| Fracture Type | Expected Flora | Prophylaxis Coverage |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Closed facial fracture** | Skin: *S. aureus*, *S. epidermidis*, *Streptococcus* | Gram-positive (1st gen cephalosporin) |
| **Open mandible** (dentate) | Oral + skin: Anaerobes (*Prevotella*, *Peptostreptococcus*), *Streptococcus*, *S. aureus* | GP + GN + anaerobes (ampicillin/sulbactam ± metronidazole) |
| **LeFort/midfacial** | Sinus flora (*S. pneumoniae*, *H. influenzae*, anaerobes) + skin | GP + some GN (*Haemophilus*) |
| **Posterior table frontal sinus** | CSF flora + sinus: GP cocci, GN bacilli, anaerobes | GP + GN + CNS penetration (ceftriaxone preferred) |
| **Mammalian bite** | Mixed: *S. aureus*, streptococci, anaerobes, *Capnocytophaga*, *Eikenella*, *Pasteurella* | GP + anaerobes (amoxicillin-clavulanate) |

**Risk Stratification for Prophylaxis Duration**

**Tier 1 (≤24h prophylaxis sufficient)**

- Closed, non-operative facial fractures
- Closed, operative facial fractures (any location)
- Open mandibular fractures (ORIF or closed reduction-MMF)
- Simple facial/scalp lacerations in healthy patients

**Tier 2 (≤24h prophylaxis; high-risk patient factors)**

- Diabetes: Increased organ space SSI risk
- Smoking: 2–3x higher SSI/deep infection risk
- Immunosuppression (steroid, biologics, HIV): Extend consideration (still ≤24h per guidelines; no RCT evidence for longer)
- Obesity: May require higher cephalosporin doses; pharmacokinetics affected
- Extremes of age: (>75 yrs or <18 yrs): Consider renal dosing; pediatrics may have lower SSI

**Tier 3 (Special circumstances; consult trauma surgeon/ID)**

- Open contaminated mandible + immunosuppression
- Posterior table frontal sinus + CSF leak
- GSW to face + contamination
- Bites (mammalian): 3–5 days standard
- Clostridial contamination (farm): High-dose penicillin regardless of fracture type

**Timing of Antibiotic Administration**

- **Prophylactic**: Administer within **1 hour** of incision (or **2 hours** for vancomycin/fluoroquinolones)
- **Perioperative**: Redose if surgery exceeds 2 half-lives of antibiotic (cefazolin: redose q4h if long case)
- **Delayed presentation** (>24h from injury): If surgery not immediate, some protocols continue antibiotics q6h until OR (but data limited; AAST allows either approach)
- **Redosing in ICU**: Not needed if <24h total; discontinue at 24h post-op regardless of ongoing sedation/ventilation

**Tier 1B \& 2 Evidence (Recent Meta-Analyses \& Systematic Reviews)**

**2024 SISNA Meta-Analysis** (Mandibular fractures ORIF)[^2_14]

- **24–72h vs. <24h prophylaxis**: OR 0.85 (95% CI 0.62–1.17) [no difference]
- **>72h vs. <24h**: OR 1.42 (95% CI 0.96–2.11); after outlier removal: **1.63 (95% CI 1.08–2.46)** [**HIGHER infection with prolonged**]
- **Conclusion**: No evidence prolonged PAP reduces SSI; >72h may paradoxically increase SSI

**2025 Goormans Meta-Review**[^2_15][^2_16][^2_17]

- 29 articles screened; 10 eligible (3 with low ROBIS bias)
- **Finding**: Low-bias SRs found NO support for prolonged antibiotic treatment after surgical intervention of midfacial fractures vs. ≤24h
- **Conclusion**: Restricted duration (≤24h) recommended; future trials needed

**2025 FRI Meta-Analysis**[^2_18]

- **1-day vs. 5-day amoxicillin-clavulanate (mandible RCT)**: 12% vs. 16% (p=NS)
- **Pediatric**: 0.9% with AB vs. 0.5% without (p=0.12; aOR 1.1, 95% CI 0.53–2.34)
- **Conclusion**: Amoxicillin-clavulanate ≤24h is standard; no advantage to extension

**2025 Rational Utilization (Maxillofacial)**[^2_19]

- 26 studies on mandibular/dentoalveolar fractures
- **Preoperative AB**: Strongly reduces infections (Level 1)
- **Postoperative >24h**: NO substantial advantage (Level 2B)
- Pooled SSI: 5.6% overall (range 1–33%)

| **Clindamycin** | 600–900mg IV q6–8h | Lincosamide (protein synthesis) | GP cocci, excellent anaerobic coverage; POOR GN coverage | **Penicillin-allergic patients** (non-IgE); covers anaerobes well; risk *C. difficile*; resistance increasing |

| Agent | Reason |
| :-- | :-- |
| **Fluoroquinolones** (levofloxacin, ciprofloxacin) | EAST Level 1: No advantage vs. cephalosporin/aminoglycoside; may impair fracture healing; higher infection rates in Type III open fractures; overused in practice |

| **Aminoglycosides** (gentamicin, amikacin) | For extremity Gustilo fractures, not facial; nephrotoxicity/ototoxicity concerns; not recommended for routine facial trauma prophylaxis |

- **eGFR >60**: Standard dosing
- **eGFR 30–59**: Cefazolin q12h (not q8h); ceftriaxone q24h (not q12h); ampicillin/sulbactam q8–12h
- **eGFR <30**: Consult nephrology; accumulation risk; consider vancomycin

### 8. CONTROVERSIES \& ACTIVE DEBATES

**1. Mandibular Angle Fractures: Role of Antibiotic Prophylaxis**

- **Debate**: Classic literature (Chole 1987) suggested benefit; 2 recent mandible RCTs (Campos, Perepa) show benefit of ≥ 24h vs. single-dose
- **Current Evidence**: No RCT compares 24h to no antibiotics specifically for angle fractures; extrapolating from Chole's 1987 data suggests benefit
- **Recommendation**: AAST/SIS consensus supports ≤24h for open mandible (including angle); single-dose for closed
- **Unresolved**: Does non-operative closed angle fracture need prophylaxis? (SIS says no; some surgeons give 24h empirically)

**2. Immunosuppressed Patients: Should Prophylaxis Be Extended?**

- **Debate**: Steroid use, HIV, biologics (TNF inhibitors) theoretically increase infection risk
- **Evidence**: Limited data; NSQIP analysis found no difference in SSI by immunosuppression status in facial fracture cohort
- **Current Guidelines**: AAST recommends still ≤24h even in high-risk patients (no RCT data for extension)
- **Unresolved**: Is ≤24h adequate in transplant patients, severe immunosuppression? (Practice varies; ID consultation often obtained)

**3. Frontal Sinus Posterior Table Fractures: When to Use CNS-Penetrating Antibiotics?**

- **Debate**: Risk of meningitis ~1% even without prophylaxis (Cochrane meta-analysis); posterior table fractures have dural exposure
- **Evidence**: No RCT data on antibiotic type for frontal sinus; some institutions use ceftriaxone (better CNS penetration), others cefazolin
- **Current Guidelines**: AAST recommends ≤24h (type unspecified); UAB/Vanderbilt protocols use ampicillin/sulbactam x24h or ceftriaxone (broader GN/CNS)
- **Unresolved**: Cephalosporin generation (1st vs. 3rd) for posterior table? (Expert opinion suggests 3rd-gen preferred, but no RCT)

**4. CSF Leak Management: Is Antibiotic Prophylaxis Ever Justified?**

- **Debate**: Meningitis risk ~10% with CSF leak; prophylactic antibiotics appear not to reduce this (Cochrane 2015)
- **Evidence**: Strong: 5 RCTs (Eftekhar, Hoff, Demetriades, Klastersky, others) found NO difference in meningitis incidence with/without prophylaxis
- **Current Guidelines**: AAST/SIS strong recommendation AGAINST prophylactic antibiotics; focus on early repair
- **Unresolved**: Should IV antibiotics be given only if meningitis develops (treatment, not prophylaxis)? (Yes, consensus)

**5. Delayed Presentation (>24h): Continue Antibiotics Until Surgery or Stop?**

- **Debate**: If patient presents 48h post-injury and hasn't received prophylaxis, should antibiotics be continued until surgery?
- **Evidence**: No RCT data; AAST allows either approach (start prophylaxis at admission if surgery planned; or defer until pre-operative)
- **Current Practice**: Mixed; many trauma centers give 24h prophylaxis from admission (q6h until OR), others from incision only
- **Unresolved**: Optimal timing; no high-quality evidence; expert opinion varies

**6. Conservatively Managed Facial Fractures: Any Role for Prophylaxis?**

- **Debate**: Open/contaminated mandible managed non-operatively (wire fixation, elastic bands) — do antibiotics reduce infection?
- **Evidence**: Weak; Malekpour 2016 RCT found 0% infection in all groups (no AB, 1–5 days, >5 days)
- **Current Guidelines**: SIS recommends AGAINST prophylactic antibiotics for non-operative fractures (strong recommendation)
- **Unresolved**: Subset analyses suggest possible benefit for very high-risk patients, but insufficient data; most avoid prophylaxis

**7. Cephalosporin vs. Ampicillin/Sulbactam for Open Mandible: Superiority?**

- **Debate**: Ampicillin/sulbactam covers anaerobes; cefazolin does not. Is combination better?
- **Evidence**: No comparative RCT in facial trauma; cefazolin + metronidazole historically used; ampicillin/sulbactam more convenient (monotherapy)
- **Current Guidelines**: AAST lists all as equivalent (Tier 1 choices)
- **Unresolved**: Should cefazolin + metronidazole be preferred over monotherapy ampicillin/sulbactam? (Expert opinion: probably equivalent; ampicillin/sulbactam more practical for compliance)

**8. Smoking, Diabetes, Obesity: Antibiotic Dose/Duration Adjustments?**

- **Debate**: High-risk patients may warrant higher doses or longer prophylaxis
- **Evidence**: NSQIP analysis: smoking/diabetes increase SSI but antibiotic duration >24h does not reduce risk in these groups
- **Current Guidelines**: No dose/duration adjustments recommended in AAST/SIS; standard dosing applies; focus on modifiable factors (glycemic control, cessation counseling)
- **Unresolved**: Should obese patients (BMI >40) receive higher cephalosporin doses? (Pharmacokinetics may be altered; some institutions use ideal body weight + 40%; no RCT)

### 9. CLINICAL PEARLS FOR LEVEL 1 TRAUMA CENTER

**DO (Evidence-Based)**

1. **Administer prophylactic antibiotics WITHIN 1 HOUR of incision** (2 hours for vancomycin/quinolones)—compliance with this timing is associated with lower SSI[^2_12][^2_11]
2. **Limit duration to ≤24 hours post-operatively** for ALL facial fracture types (mandible, midfacial, upper)—prolongation provides no benefit and may increase SSI[^2_14]
3. **For OPEN/CONTAMINATED mandibular fractures**: Use ampicillin/sulbactam 3g IV q6h or cefazolin 1–2g q8h (+ metronidazole if anaerobic coverage desired)—equivalent to single-dose at 24h[^2_9]
4. **For CLOSED/NON-CONTAMINATED operative facial fractures**: Preoperative dose only; no post-operative extension—single-dose at induction may suffice for midfacial[^2_14]
5. **Assess for oral cavity communication**—if present, mandate antibiotic prophylaxis ≤24h (consider anaerobic coverage with ampicillin/sulbactam or cefazolin + metronidazole)
6. **Early surgical intervention** (within 24 hours of injury)—infection rates 4.2% (<24h) vs. 55% (>7d); prioritize OR scheduling
7. **Skin antisepsis + wound irrigation** (≥500 mL NS, debridement of devitalized tissue)—integral to infection prevention alongside antibiotics
8. **Adjust dosing for renal function**—cefazolin q12h if eGFR 30–59; consult renal dosing apps
9. **Counsel smoking cessation** perioperatively—smokers have 2–3x higher SSI/deep infection risk; even brief counseling effective
10. **Check patient's drug allergy history BEFORE prophylaxis**—avoid cephalosporins in anaphylaxis history; use clindamycin in non-IgE penicillin allergy

**DO NOT (Evidence Against)**

1. **Do NOT routinely extend prophylaxis beyond 24 hours**—meta-analysis (2024) shows >72h vs. <24h increases SSI (OR 1.63, 95% CI 1.08–2.46)[^2_14]
2. **Do NOT give antibiotics for closed, non-operative facial fractures**—no evidence of benefit; increases C. difficile and resistance risk[^2_13]
3. **Do NOT prescribe fluoroquinolones** for routine facial trauma prophylaxis—EAST Level 1 evidence against; may impair healing; no advantage over cephalosporins[^2_20]
4. **Do NOT give prophylactic antibiotics for basilar skull fractures ± CSF leak**—Cochrane meta-analysis: no reduction in meningitis with prophylaxis; focus on early repair + isolation[^2_10]
5. **Do NOT delay surgery waiting for "optimal timing" of antibiotics**—preoperative dose within 1h is sufficient; early OR access more important than perfect timing
6. **Do NOT use aminoglycosides for facial fracture prophylaxis**—reserved for Gustilo Type III extremity fractures; nephrotoxicity/ototoxicity not warranted for facial trauma
7. **Do NOT routinely extend prophylaxis in immunosuppressed patients**—insufficient data; AAST recommends ≤24h even in high-risk groups
8. **Do NOT prescribe prophylactic antibiotics for simple facial/scalp lacerations** (without contamination, tissue destruction, or high-risk patient factors)—low baseline infection risk (<1%); risks (allergy, C. difficile) outweigh benefit
9. **Do NOT redose antibiotics post-operatively** if total course is ≤24h—discontinue at 24h regardless of ongoing sedation/ICU status
10. **Do NOT ignore oral contamination risk**—dentate mandible injuries (angle, body, symphysis, parasymphysis) presumed OPEN; mandate anaerobic coverage

**Pearls on Oral Flora \& Odontogenic Infection**

- **Dental status matters**: Teeth with caries at fracture line → higher infection risk; severely compromised teeth may need extraction
- **Periodontal disease**: Poor gum health (P4 periodontitis) associated with higher infection rates; preoperative chlorhexidine rinse may reduce flora load
- **Intraoral lacerations (through-and-through)**: Require antibiotics ≤24h AND careful intraoral repair (resorbable sutures); avoid leaving gaps
- **Dentoalveolar fractures**: Tooth loss with fracture may affect infection; consultation with oral surgeon recommended

**Pearls on Complications**

- **Late-onset infection**: Can present 4–6 weeks post-op (peak ~3 weeks); follow-up at 2–4 weeks essential; some infections not apparent at 30-day mark
- **Mandibular infections often present as localized abscess** requiring I\&D; do NOT reflexively remove hardware unless chronic/recurrent
- **Non-union risk**: Increases with smoking, infection, high-energy trauma, poor reduction; ensure rigid fixation if ORIF planned
- **TMJ dysfunction**: Common after mandibular trauma (especially condylar); early physiotherapy/jaw exercises important; referral to PM\&R if persistent

### 10. CONTROVERSIES \& RESEARCH GAPS

**Data Limitations Identified**

1. **Low-Quality Evidence Overall**: Exception is strong recommendation AGAINST prolonged therapy; most recommendations are weak due to small RCT sample sizes, short follow-up, and heterogeneous study designs
2. **Pediatric Bias**: Many included RCTs enrolled patients <18 years (superior infection resistance); results may overestimate efficacy in adult trauma
3. **Oral Flora Characterization Lacking**: No recent studies document causative organisms (anaerobes vs. aerobes) in facial fracture infections; unable to optimize antibiotic selection based on microbiology
4. **Long-Term Follow-Up Insufficient**: Most studies follow ≤6 weeks; infections can present 8–12 weeks post-op (average ~3 weeks); may underestimate true infection rates
5. **Standardized Infection Definitions Absent**: CDC-SSI criteria used variably; some studies use clinical criteria only (no culture confirmation); limits comparability
6. **Comorbidity Substudies Needed**: No large RCT specifically evaluating antibiotic duration in diabetics, smokers, or immunosuppressed subgroups
7. **Antibiotic Type Comparison Absent**: No RCT directly compares cefazolin vs. ceftriaxone vs. ampicillin/sulbactam in facial trauma; equivalent efficacy assumed but not proven

**Future Research Priorities**

1. Adequately powered, prospective RCT comparing:
    - Open mandibular fractures: Cefazolin monotherapy vs. ampicillin/sulbactam vs. cefazolin + metronidazole (all ≤24h)
    - Outcome: SSI rate, infection severity, organism species, time to infection onset
    - Follow-up: ≥12 months
2. Subgroup analyses by:
    - Smoking status (never, former, active)
    - Glycemic control (non-diabetic, pre-diabetic, diabetic with A1C <7% vs. >7%)
    - Immunosuppression (on/off steroids, biologic use)
    - Renal function (eGFR categories)
3. Observational study documenting microbiology of facial fracture infections:
    - Culture/PCR of infected sites
    - Identify predictive factors for anaerobic vs. aerobic infection
    - Guide future antibiotic selection
4. Comparative effectiveness research (CER) on:
    - Single-dose vs. ≤24h vs. prolonged prophylaxis in population-based cohort
    - C. difficile and resistant organism acquisition
    - Economic impact (cost-effectiveness analysis)
5. Immunological studies on:
    - Why CSF leak meningitis rate unchanged with/without prophylaxis (CSF-immune privilege? inadequate CNS penetration?)
    - Early inflammatory response biomarkers predicting infection risk

### 11. INSTITUTIONAL PROTOCOL EXAMPLE

**VANDERBILT UNIVERSITY MEDICAL CENTER PROTOCOL (2022–2024)**[^2_21]

**Open-Facial Fractures (Blunt or Penetrating)**

- Preoperative: Ampicillin/sulbactam 3g IV 30 minutes before incision
- Intraoperative: 1–2 additional doses (depends on case length; redose q4h if >2h)
- Postoperative: Total 3 doses, discontinue (= ~18h total)
- **Do NOT continue >24 hours post-operatively**
- If no immediate OR: Ampicillin/sulbactam 3g IV q6h for 24 hours; administer routine perioperative dose on day of surgery

**Closed Facial Fractures (Operative)**

- Preoperative: Ampicillin/sulbactam 3g IV 30 minutes before incision OR Cefazolin 2g IV
- Postoperative: **NONE**—single perioperative dose sufficient

**Posterior Table Frontal Sinus Fractures**

- Preoperative: Ampicillin/sulbactam 3g IV 30 minutes before incision
- Intraoperative/Postoperative: Continue for 3 total doses (~18–24h)
- Rationale: CSF exposure; improved GN/anaerobic coverage vs. cefazolin

**Open Mandible Fractures**

- Same as open-facial fractures above (ampicillin/sulbactam preferred for oral flora coverage)

**Non-operative Facial Fractures**

- **NO prophylactic antibiotics**

**Allergy Accommodations**

- **IgE-mediated penicillin allergy**: Clindamycin 600mg IV q6h x3 doses
- **Non-IgE penicillin allergy (rash history)**: Cephalosporin acceptable (1–3% cross-reactivity); use if no other option
- **Cephalosporin allergy**: Vancomycin 15–20 mg/kg IV q8h x3 doses

**Significant Risk Factors (Univariate + Multivariate)**[^2_22]

- **Smoking**: 2.3x higher risk deep SSI (p<0.05); 2.1x higher organ space SSI (p<0.05)
- **Diabetes**: 1.5x higher organ space SSI in mandibular fractures (p<0.05)
- **Immediate/emergent surgery**: 1.8x higher superficial SSI in ZMC fractures (p<0.05)
- **Immediate surgery (all)**: 2.2x higher organ space SSI; 1.7x higher reoperation (p<0.05)
- **Age, BMI, gender**: Not significantly associated with 30-day SSI in facial fracture cohort

**Delayed Infection Presentation**[^2_9]

- Average time to diagnosis: **3 weeks post-op** (range 1–12 weeks)
- 64% of infections diagnosed by 90-day follow-up; 89% by 1 year
- **Implication**: Short (30-day) follow-up periods in RCTs may underestimate true infection rates

## ACTIONABLE SUMMARY FOR RESIDENT HANDOFF

**On-Call Trauma Resident Receives Facial Fracture Patient at 2 AM**

1. **First question**: "Is this fracture OPEN (communication to oral cavity, skin laceration, or penetrating injury)?" → YES → mandate antibiotics ≤24h
    - If CLOSED/NON-OPERATIVE → NO prophylactic antibiotics
2. **Second question**: "What is the site (mandible vs. upper/midfacial)?"
    - Mandible (dentate segment) = assume OPEN; even closed fractures may need coverage
    - Upper/midfacial = closed until proven otherwise
3. **Drug selection** (all equivalent; pick one):
    - **Open/contaminated OR open mandible**: Ampicillin/sulbactam 3g IV q6h × 3 doses (~18h) OR cefazolin 2g IV q8h + metronidazole 500mg q8h
    - **Closed operative**: Cefazolin 2g IV q8h × 1 dose at incision (no post-op)
    - **Penicillin allergy (non-anaphylaxis)**: Clindamycin 600mg IV q6h × 3 doses or cephalosporin if no cross-reactivity
4. **Timing**: WITHIN **1 HOUR** of incision (or start of surgery); if delayed >1h, still give pre-incision dose
5. **Duration**: **STOP after 24 hours post-op REGARDLESS of ICU status, sedation, or clinical improvement**
6. **Do NOT**:
    - Give fluoroquinolones (no benefit; may impair healing)
    - Extend beyond 24h (increases C. difficile, resistance, SSI risk)
    - Give antibiotics for closed non-operative fractures
    - Give antibiotics for basilar skull fractures without meningitis signs
7. **Tell the surgeon**: "Per AAST/SIS 2024 guidelines, prophylaxis is ≤24h; no evidence for prolongation. Early OR access more important than perfect antibiotic timing."
